English Literature

Old English Period (428-1066)

General Information

The Old English Period, also known as the Anglo-Saxon period, includes all literature written during the Anglo-Saxon period of Britain. The majority of surviving works were written during the last three centuries of the era. The end of this period is marked by the Norman Conquest of 1066.

This period spawned largely out of necessity, as there was a lack of Latin literacy after the Danish invasions, so a significant part of the population was unable to read Latin works. King Alfred the Great, the king of the southern Anglo-Saxon kingdom of Wessex from 871 to 899, noticed that many Englishmen were able to read Old English but were unable to read Latin. Thus, he proposed that students would first learn Old English.

There is a shockingly low amount of surviving works, there being only 3,037 texts, adding up to about 3 million words.

Language and Structure

Old English is the earliest form of the English language. It takes strong influence from Latin, borrowing many words. The language developed from Germanic dialects spoken by invaders in the 5th and 6th centuries. While Middle English is generally understandable in the modern day, it is far more difficult to understand the words and syntax of Old English. As a language, it was in use until the Norman conquest, when for a short time, French became the primary means of written communication.

As stated previously, Old English is strongly influenced by Latin. As Britain was formerly occupied by the Romans prior to this period, and aristocrats may have continued to use the language for communication, a significant portion of Old English vocabulary is borrowed from Latin. Additionally, once Saint Augustine arrived, monk influence increased the amount of Latin that got interspliced with the language. A tactic that would be used was creating compound words, combining multiple Germanic pieces to represent the meaning of a Latin word.

Caedmon's Hymn is the earliest example of a poem written in English. It was originally an oral hymn composed by a cowherd named Caedmon. Later, it was put into writing by a monk named Bede. It has the style of 4-beat lines consistent with other English poetry.
